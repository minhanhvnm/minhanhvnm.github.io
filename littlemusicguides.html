Publication Design

Little Music Guides —

Three lighthearted real-time listening guides to three pieces of classical music, accompanied by a transcription of a famous 1950s TV broadcast on classical music for young children.

Classical music is usually described as “calming” or “classy”—totally different from the energy and color and emotion that a musician might hear in a classical piece. I was curious as to whether there was a way to help non-musicians understand the color and drama in classical music as vividly as musicians do.

Date — Fall 2016
Class — Publication Design
Instructor — Jayme Yen (from Schema)

The volumes are for three different pieces—“Spring” by Vivaldi, “Danse Macabre” by Saint-Saens, and “Passepied” by Debussy. I also added a fourth volume; a transcription of a Young People's Concert by Leonard Bernstein. Finally, I made a Tumblr to provide a collection of the relevant audio, crafted especially for this listening experience.

Initial Ideas

My first ideas focused on the idea that there are multiple ways to appreciate a piece of music, and that different people can approach a piece and take away totally different interpretations, based on some writing on listening to classical music written by music critics and composers.

I found that in my initial ideation, the ideas that I came up with were pretty abstract. People I talked to didn't find the concept compelling, as the ideas didn't address a really basic experience that non-musicians have—classical music is simply difficult to listen to.

Prototype, Round 1

I started thinking more about the listening experience, and how I might make the experience more accessible.

My first prototype was a picture book with a whimsical, preschool-friendly story I made up that listeners could flip through as they listened to the piece.

The story is accompanied by timestamps, and brief descriptions and snippets of the music tucked between the pages.

People who tested my paper prototype found the picture book format fun, light-hearted, and accessible.

But, I also realized that the French fold technique was too cumbersome to be usable—users often got lost between all the different types of information. Some of that information, like the score, didn’t contribute to the experience at all.

Prototype, Round 2

I got rid of the French fold technique, putting it all on one page—the story, the timestamp, the description. I also added “turn the page” cues, so that the listener would not have to constant refer back to the timestamps to know when to turn the page.

Listeners who I tested both prototypes on described the newer one as more immersive.

Final Product

I used a cream-colored, thicker paper, emulating paper you might find in a music book, and tied the whole thing together with twine (the twine/hole combination reminded me of stringed instruments). I used glossy paper for the outside paper plaques to contrast with the soft finish of the printed covers.
